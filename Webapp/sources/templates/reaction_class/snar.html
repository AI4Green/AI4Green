SNAr & SN2

Nucleophilic substitution is a reaction mechanism whereby a nucleophile replaces a functional group in an electrophilic molecule, usually in the presence of a base. Nucleophilic aromatic substitution (SNAr) and bimolecular nucleophilic substitution (SN2) are sub-categories of nucleophilic substitution.





SNAr and SN2 reactions proceed via charged intermediates or transition states which can be stabilised by basic, polar aprotic solvents. These reactions are also accelerated by poorly solvated nucleophiles, so solvents with fewer hydrogen bonding groups are favoured. Experimental Data

SNAr
A report by Cseri et al.,1 investigated a brief solvent screen for the SNAr reaction of bis(4-fluorophenyl) sulfone with phenol. The data for this screen can be viewed by selecting "SNAr" in the Change Colour dropdown.



SN2
Similar solvent effects have been seen for SN2 reactions. Work by Schleicher et al.,2 demonstrated that for the synthesis of ionic liquids, solvents with higher Kamlet-Taft π* and α parameters enhanced the reaction rates, while solvent with higher Kamlet-Taft β parameters were detrimental.
