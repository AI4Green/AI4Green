Heck Cross-coupling

The Heck reaction is a type of transition metal-catalysed cross-coupling that forms a carbon-carbon bond between an alkene and an aryl halide. These reactions are usually catalysed by palladium and the choice of ligand, base and solvent can affect both productivity and selectivity.




In the presence of ligand, the rate-determining step of the reaction mechanism is the palladium insertion into the alkene bond,1 which leads to a significant build-up of charge. Polar solvents that can stabilise this charge lead to an increase in the initial rate,2 which shows a dependence on the dipolarity (Kamlet-Taft π*) of the solvent. Conversely, protic solvents lead to a decrease in the initial rate.

A previous report by Burello et al.3 employed PCA for the optimisation of the Heck reaction, and found that solvent polarity and basicity were important for an effective description of solvent space. As such, descriptors that capture these properties were chosen for this Heck PCA map.

Experimental Data

A report by Sangon et al.4 explored the use of greener solvents for the Heck reaction of iodobenzene with methylacrylate and styrene. The conversions of these reactions in each solvent was averaged and this data can be viewed by selecting "Heck" in the Colour Change dropdown.
