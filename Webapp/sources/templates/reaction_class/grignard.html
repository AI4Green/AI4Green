Grignard Addition

Grignard reagents are highly reactive nucleophiles formed from the oxidative addition of elemental magnesium across a carbon-halide bond. These reagents are air and moisture sensitive, and are often reacted with electrophiles such as ketones via nucleophilic addition.





Grignard reagents are usually prepared in ethereal solvents (THF, Et2O) which can stabilise the magnesium centre by coordination, though highly basic solvents have been shown to reduce the rates of Grignard addition reactions.1 This effect is also seen with polar solvents, which can raise the activation energy of the addition step by stabilising the organometallic reagent.2 Protic solvents are also detrimental, as these can lead to violent quenching of the Grignard species. These considerations mean that aprotic, basic solvents with medium polarity are ideal for these reactions, and the descriptor set for PCA was chosen accordingly.
